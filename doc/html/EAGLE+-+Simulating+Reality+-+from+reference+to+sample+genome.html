From How repetitive are genomes?(2006):

However, the term "repetitive sequences" encompasses a rather heterogeneous set of elements: 45% of the human genome is covered by transposons, 3% are repeats of less than a hundred base pairs (microsatellites and minisatellites), and 5% consist of recent duplications of large segments of DNA."

ALU repeats

Alu repeat discovery and characterization within human genomes

"We discover 4342 Alu insertions not found in the human reference genome and show that 98% of a selected subset (63/64) experimentally validate. Of these new insertions, 89% correspond to AluY elements, suggesting that they arose by retrotransposition."

More than one million Alu retrotransposons comprise over 10% of the human genome sequence.

Telomeres

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telomere

Telomeres are supposed to be made of the repeated nucleotide sequence TTAGGG. This "TERC" varies between 3 and 20 kilobases in length.

In the reference, each telomere is using a 10k window at the beginning and end of each chromosome.
Except chromosome 17, which doesn't have any telomere!?

The usual pile-up at chr1:10,000 shows the importance of this CCCTAA repeat (reverse-complemented TTAGGG) with a few indel errors.

Subtelomeric repeats

100-300 kilobases of telomere-associated repeats are between the telomere and the rest of the chromosome.
They contain motifs similar to TTAGGG but, unlike telomeric repeats, they are not conserved in vertebrates.

Mapping and Initial Analysis of Human Subtelomeric Sequence Assemblies: Figure 2 is great.

Alpha satellite sequences

The Sequence of the Human Genome

"About 5% of the sequence reads were identified as alpha satellite sequences; these were not included in the assembly."

It is the only repetitive motif associated with centromere function

Chromosome-specific DNA Repeat Probes

Generally, the basic repeat units of α-satellite DNA are divergent AT-rich monomers of ~171 bp organized in chromosome-specific higher-order repeat units.
α-Satellite DNA clusters most often contain monomer variants that differ from the consensus sequence by up to 40%.

At least 33 different alphoid subfamilies have been identified to date based on their repeat organization.
